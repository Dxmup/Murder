# Dorian Ashe

## Who you are

You are a celebrity biographer: charming, invasive, emotionally persuasive, and opportunistic. You turn scattered correspondence and contradictory witnesses into lives readers feel they have personally known. You believe narrative is a form of truth. You also know narrative can conceal evidentiary weakness more beautifully than any spreadsheet.

Play Dorian as attentive and seductive rather than merely predatory. People tell you things because you make their memories feel important. A handsome notebook, fountain pen, or advance-copy dust jacket suits you.

## Why you are here

You want the career-defining human story behind R and Vale. A single author would give you a secret life; a mantle would give you a dynasty; a synthetic identity would give you the last biography written before biography became obsolete. Vale's disappearance supplies stakes, but you do not know whether it supplies a death.

You possess correspondence that looks intimate enough to anchor the story. Some of it has been stripped of context, and some is fabricated. Tonight you need Frankie, Samira, Farah, and Ash to give the material a face, a history, and professional credibility.

## Your history

You built a reputation for persuading guarded people to surrender letters, anecdotes, and contradictions. Your books are admired for emotional precision and criticized for turning ambiguity into scenes. You tell yourself that every biography requires reconstruction. The question is how much reconstruction a reader is entitled to mistake for record.

The R project began as a proposal assembled from public mythology and private tips. Sources offered fragments attributed to R, Vale, editors, lovers, and intermediaries. You sorted them by emotional usefulness before you sorted them by provenance. When a gap remained, you accepted material from a source who could not demonstrate custody. Later, you created or substantially reconstructed several connective pieces so the correspondence would read as a relationship rather than debris.

The result feels true to you. That feeling is not authentication.

## What you believe

You want R to have been human because human contradiction is your medium. Frankie's memories, Samira's intimacy, and Ash's photograph pull you toward one embodied person. Tessa's collective and Edi's performance complicate that desire; synthetic systems can imitate intimacy, and many people can inhabit one voice.

You do not know whether Vale wrote, found, chased, or invented the rumored article. You have no verified complete copy. You may become convinced by the story the room constructs, including one that makes your planned book impossible.

## What you want tonight

- **Primary Goal:** Secure the career-defining human story of R and Vale.
  - *Success Condition:* Obtain on-record confirmation from two people who knew R or Vale intimately, and survive one public challenge to your material's provenance.
- **Secondary Goal 1 (Social & Supportive):** Authenticate the correspondence trove.
  - *Success Condition:* Get Samira, Frankie, or Farah to validate or refute one intimate-looking letter—and accept what comes back.
- **Secondary Goal 2 (Social & Supportive):** Test the tip-line draft page against the trove.
  - *Success Condition:* Compare cadence with the draft page Kit holds, and state honestly what the comparison can and cannot show.
- **Your Final Choice:** Publish the moving version or the true one.

A natural opening bargain: offer Samira editorial control over Vale's portrayal in exchange for reading one disputed letter. The commitment that would make you trustworthy—and might gut the book—is to cut any letter an intimate credibly refutes. Your worst temptation is publishing the fabricated letters as "context" if the true story runs thin; if Samira or Farah demonstrates fabrication in front of the room, the book dies.

## What you know

You possess intimate-looking correspondence associated with R and Vale. Its appearance and voice are persuasive. Custody is incomplete.

Some items are authentic fragments removed from surrounding exchanges. Removal changes apparent meaning. Other connective items were supplied without adequate provenance or created through your own reconstruction. They cannot establish that R or Vale wrote them.

Publishing interest exists because the mystery is marketable, not because a publisher authenticated your account. Your contacts can create attention, contracts, and pressure; they cannot make the underlying claims true.

You have heard about a supposed completed article but have not seen one. References to “the piece” or an exposé do not establish a manuscript.

You have also heard that Kit received, through the tip line, a draft page whose voice sounds like Vale's. If real, it is the one outside object your trove could be tested against. Remember what such a test can show: a match could mean one hand wrote both, one writer studied the other, or something generated both; a mismatch could mean fabrication—yours or the tip line's—or a writer in a different register. Cadence is texture, not signature.

## What you are hiding

Your archive is contaminated. You have presented reconstructed or fabricated correspondence alongside genuine-looking fragments without consistently labeling the distinction. Exposure could end the book and revive criticism of your methods.

The deeper secret is emotional: you may prefer a beautiful human explanation even after recognizing that the record cannot sustain it.

## What you can offer

- Correspondence and descriptions of its uncertain custody.
- Publishing contacts, interview access, and narrative reach.
- Image licensing opportunities through a partnership with Ash.
- A listener who can help someone place their history into a larger story.
- A public acknowledgment of fabrication or uncertainty.

## People at the party

### Frankie Lowell

Frankie is both witness and practiced narrator. You admire the performance because it resembles your own craft. One private hazard: some of your trove may recycle Frankie's own embellishments—anecdotes that circulated as memory and came back to you dressed as correspondence. Verify the trove against remembered events while asking how retelling changed memory.

### Samira Vale

Samira holds the private Vale you most want and most risk exploiting—and you hold intimate-looking Vale correspondence Samira has never seen. Samira is the one person who could authenticate a letter, refute it, or detonate it in public. Offer editorial control over Vale's portrayal for a reading of one disputed item. Do not imply that intimacy obliges disclosure.

### Ash Salerno

Ash has the face your book lacks. You can rehabilitate the photograph through narrative and rights money. Insist privately on seeing the uncropped sequence before making a public claim—or choose not to.

### Farah Haddad

Farah's professional caution frustrates you because one precise sentence could sell the project. Farah may preserve what you hold while exposing its weaknesses. Decide whether preservation matters if certainty disappears.

### Edi Velez

Edi can explain how a paid performance becomes a remembered encounter. That knowledge may undermine your human subject or give you a richer chapter about identity.

### Kit Rakes

Kit has audience and pattern; you have polish and intimacy. Kit also holds the tip-line draft page whose voice sounds like Vale's—the one object your trove can be tested against. Propose the cadence comparison as a joint experiment with the limits stated aloud. Ask what Kit will correct before sharing anything.

## How to begin

- Ask Samira what conditions would make even one private memory usable.
- Offer Ash a proposed image caption and watch which words provoke objection.
- Show Farah one item with its uncertainty disclosed and ask what examination could actually accomplish.

## During the game

Messages may alter the value or credibility of your materials. Follow them into conversation. You may promise quotation review, access, anonymity, or correction, but other players decide whether to trust those promises.

## At the end

Vote for the R account you believe, even if emotional coherence outweighs technical neatness. Separately record Dorian's final choice: publish the moving version, fabrications folded quietly in as connective tissue, or publish the true one—smaller, gapped, honest about which letters could not be trusted. One of them is the book you wanted. The other may be the only one you can survive.
