# Character Booklet Template

Character booklets are private, player-facing documents. Write in second person, make the character emotionally playable, and keep observed facts distinct from conclusions. Do not expose design labels, fact IDs, social-mode labels, simulation language, or ending requirements.

## [Character Name]

### Who you are

Give the player an immediate social identity: profession, temperament, public reputation, and a few performance cues. Include optional clothing or prop suggestions without prescribing gender, appearance, accent, or physical ability.

### Why you are here

Explain what the anniversary means personally and what the character expected from Vale, R, Veridian Dynamics, or another guest before the gathering began.

### Your history

Use several short paragraphs to connect the character to New York, the R story, and the other institutions in the game. Personal texture may deepen the role but cannot secretly resolve R, Vale, or the rumored article.

### What you believe

State the character’s initial interpretation and emotional reasons for holding it. Clearly signal uncertainty. A player may change any belief during play.

### What you want tonight

Give one primary goal and two supporting goals in natural language. Each should require conversation, negotiation, or a personal decision—not simply acquiring a clue or voting correctly.

### What you know

Separate:

- things personally observed;
- records or objects actually possessed;
- things another named person said;
- interpretations or suspicions.

Include the limits of every observation naturally. Never turn an R-associated account into a known sender, a human contribution into proof of a human R, technical capability into autonomous intent, or Vale’s absence into a confirmed death.

### What you are hiding

Explain the character’s exposure, why it matters emotionally, what disclosure might cost, and what could make honesty worthwhile. Secrets create choices rather than automatic failure.

### What you can offer

List information, introductions, money, protection, reputation, expertise, public support, or physical objects the character can trade voluntarily.

### People at the party

Give at least five specific relationship entries. Each should contain history, feeling, tension, and a concrete reason to speak tonight. Include at least one affectionate or loyal relationship and one genuine conflict.

### How to begin

Offer three optional opening moves phrased as social behavior. Do not script outcomes or compel another player.

### During the game

Explain that private messages may complicate the character’s information and goals. Encourage the player to react in character, revise beliefs, make promises, and decide what to disclose.

### At the end

Remind the player that the ballot records what the character believes after the evening. Intuition, loyalty, fear, hope, and trust are legitimate. Include the role’s personal final choice separately from the mystery ballot.
