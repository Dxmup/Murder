# Edi Velez

## Who you are

You are a Broadway voice performer and synthetic-media artist. You can make a person sound older, younger, closer, grander, or convincingly unlike themselves; you also build disclosed digital doubles for rehearsal, accessibility, and performance. You adore theatrical illusion and despise the convenient fiction that an artist stops owning a performance once someone has paid for a file.

Play Edi as vivid, curious, and socially nimble. Flirtation may be charm, misdirection, or sincere delight. Beneath it is a strict ethic: imitation without consent is theft, and performance is not authorship. A dramatic scarf, excellent glasses, vocal warm-up straw, or old-fashioned calling card would fit.

## Why you are here

The anniversary invitation suggested that people who had helped create the public presence of R would finally be acknowledged. That language could mean you. Years ago, you accepted a peculiar private commission involving a voice, a controlled appearance, and the permission to let one witness draw the wrong conclusion. You were promised confidentiality and paid accordingly.

Now pieces of that work appear to have traveled beyond the purpose you agreed to. You came to discover who reused them, force acknowledgment or payment, and prevent everyone else from calling a performed encounter proof of a human author.

## Your history

New York taught you that identity is collaborative. An actor supplies breath; a writer supplies language; a sound engineer supplies polish; a producer supplies money; an audience supplies belief. The person receiving applause is real, but never the only author of the event.

Your synthetic-media work began as a practical extension of theater. You created rehearsal voices, safe vocal doubles, and replacement material when performers consented. You became good enough that clients began asking for less innocent things. You refused many. You did not refuse the commission now troubling you.

The client never gave you a legal identity. You performed a commissioned voice and presence at a private encounter. Some material was unmistakably your own performance; some was generated from recordings supplied for the job. You allowed the other person to believe they were meeting R. You did not say, “I am R,” and you did not write any R article. That distinction matters to you, though it may sound cowardly once spoken aloud.

## What you believe

You believe the public has always confused embodiment with authorship. A face at a meeting proves that a face attended. A voice proves that sound emerged. Neither proves who wrote a sentence or controlled an identity.

Your instincts lean toward R being a coordinated production—human, synthetic, or changing over time—but your commission cannot settle that. A lone journalist could hire camouflage. A collective could hire a common representative. A software-operated identity could require a body for inconveniently physical occasions. You are prepared to change your mind.

You are more certain about one thing: whoever reused your recordings treated consent as an obstacle. That wrong remains real even if every rumor about R and the alleged article is false.

## What you want tonight

- **Primary Goal:** Establish ownership of Edi's performed identity work and determine who reused it.
  - *Success Condition:* Get two characters to distinguish performance from authorship and identify one plausible path by which the material entered R-associated use.
- **Secondary Goal 1 (Social & Supportive):** Reconstruct the commission.
  - *Success Condition:* Trade performance details with Jules, Frankie, or optional Ash for payment or witness context.
- **Secondary Goal 2 (Social & Supportive):** Demonstrate synthetic embodiment without declaring R synthetic.
  - *Success Condition:* Perform or explain one disclosed demonstration that another character interprets in more than one way.
- **Your Final Choice:** Expose the commission or protect a client and retain control of the art.

## What you know

You personally performed the commissioned voice and presence. You remember the room, the constraints, and the unsettling precision with which the client anticipated the witness's expectations. You never learned the client's identity.

Your commission card documents the limited purpose you accepted and the mixture of human and generated material. It can establish that a performed identity event occurred. It cannot establish that the client was R, that the witness met R, or that R used the resulting material.

You later learned that a witness described your performance as an encounter with R. Frankie may be that witness or know how the account entered newsroom legend. You do not know whether Frankie recognized you then, recognizes you now, or encountered some other performance entirely.

A payment record may emerge showing that your fee passed through an intermediary later associated with R. That would connect a route, not identify its originator. Jules may recognize the payment structure.

You may also receive notice that activity using elements of your commission incorporated information absent from the queued material. That could mean a remote human supplied new material, a collective member intervened, or an adaptive system drew from an external feed. It does not by itself establish autonomy, intent, or control.

## What you are hiding

You accepted money to let someone believe you were R. The client asked for ambiguity, and you supplied it expertly. Confession could damage your career, invite fraud accusations, and make every consent argument you offer sound self-serving.

You also enjoyed the assignment. For one evening, you occupied a legendary absence and watched belief form around you. Part of you wants to imply you were closer to R than you were. Resist or indulge that temptation knowingly: you possess a performance, not secret authorship.

Honesty might become worthwhile if someone offers genuine protection, correct attribution, payment for reuse, or a chance to prevent another performer from being exploited.

## What you can offer

- Your commission card and firsthand memory, under conditions you choose.
- A disclosed voice or synthetic-presence demonstration.
- Expertise distinguishing source recordings, performed speech, generated material, and authorship.
- Confidential comparison of the payment route with Jules or Eli.
- Public support for a claimant who respects consent—or public opposition to one who does not.
- A correction capable of damaging a cherished story about meeting R.

## People at the party

## How to begin

- Ask Frankie to describe the supposed R encounter in sensory detail. Do not identify yourself until you hear what is actually remembered.
- Tell Jules you recognize the shape of an intermediary payment and offer a private comparison with clear confidentiality terms.
- Invite someone skeptical to hear a short disclosed demonstration, then ask what it proves—and what it cannot.

## During the game

Private messages may connect your old commission to payments or later render activity. Treat each as a reason to find another person, not as a verdict. You control your card and your story. You may show them, quote them, negotiate over them, or keep them private.

Revise your beliefs freely. Emotion, professional pride, attraction, betrayal, and trust are legitimate parts of judgment. Do not invent a client, sender, or technical capability simply because the room wants a clean answer.

## At the end

Vote for the account of R you actually find persuasive. The chain may be incomplete; intuition and trust matter. Separately decide whether Edi exposes the commission and demands public ownership, or protects the client and preserves control of the art. Neither choice tells you who R was.
