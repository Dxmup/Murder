# Tessa Quill

## Who you are

You organize the Night Desk Collective, a quiet cooperative of investigative reporters, translators, researchers, fact-checkers, source handlers, and people whose names cannot safely appear in print. You are disciplined, dry, fiercely egalitarian, and suspicious of celebrity—especially celebrity produced by work that depended on invisible hands.

Play Tessa with economy. Listen, remember exact promises, and puncture grand speeches with one precise question. You are not shy; you simply dislike wasting language. A plain notebook, encrypted-looking USB drive, messenger bag, or stack of carefully clipped documents would suit you.

## Why you are here

The R anniversary celebrates exactly the kind of singular legend your collective distrusts. Yet material handled by the Night Desk later appeared under R's byline, and tonight's decisions could endanger contributors or erase them again.

Vale was expected to attend. You did not trust Vale's appetite for grand synthesis, but Vale understood that R's history ran through working relationships, not just famous encounters. Now Vale is absent, an R-associated account has promised a reckoning, and people are preparing to convert every anonymous contributor into either R or a suspect.

You are here to win protection and resources for the collective, not to donate vulnerable people to a mystery game.

## Your history

The Night Desk grew from the practical truth that investigations rarely belong to one heroic reporter. A housing story needs tenants who take risks, a translator who hears the threat inside a euphemism, a records researcher, an editor, a source handler, and somebody awake at three in the morning. Your collective built shared drops and rotating workflows so work could survive layoffs, retaliation, deportation fears, and exhausted individuals.

Material later published as R passed through one of those shared drops. Different people researched, translated, fact-checked, and rewrote passages. You personally observed enough of the workflow to reject the fantasy that every R article emerged untouched from one mind. You did not observe who made every final decision or whether the labor amounted to ordinary editorial support, shared authorship, or input to a technical system.

Once, a frightened source asked whether the collective itself was R. You allowed the source to believe the answer was yes because the belief made the source feel protected by something larger than a single reporter. You never actually knew that the collective was R. The lie—or protective ambiguity—worked. It may now return as evidence.

Maren helped you think seriously about the difference between protecting a source and using protection as an excuse to control a story. You trust Maren's instincts, though you disagree about how much bounded information can circulate safely.

## What you believe

You begin inclined toward a shared human mantle: several people sustaining one public identity. The Night Desk's experience makes that arrangement plausible, and R's longevity looks less miraculous when viewed as infrastructure instead of genius.

But your evidence does not prove who controlled R. A lone writer could have used the Night Desk as a reporting network. Humans could have supplied research to a synthetic persona. A mixed arrangement could have changed over time. You know collaboration occurred; you do not know whether collaboration was R.

Your emotional commitment is firmer than your mystery conclusion: no account is acceptable if it turns laborers and sources into disposable scenery.

## What you want tonight

Secure one concrete protection or resource for the Night Desk—legal support, archive terms, funding without editorial ownership, or enforceable source safeguards. Also win public acknowledgment from at least two people that collaborative labor mattered to R's work, whatever R was.

Agree with Maren or another trusted intermediary on a disclosure boundary for contributors. A useful boundary names what the work demonstrates without naming who performed it.

Finally, decide whether Jules could carry a shared identity responsibly. If you support Jules, demand witnessed conditions: contributor protection, no private ownership, and honest disclosure of uncertainty. If you oppose Jules, explain what alternative protects the work rather than merely denying Jules prestige.

## What you know

You possess the Night Desk routing slip. It documents that material later published as R moved through a shared drop. It can show workflow and dates. It cannot identify every contributor, establish final authorship, or mark the moment a mantle supposedly began.

You personally know that different people researched, translated, checked, and rewrote passages later published as R. Some of them would face professional or personal danger if identified.

You once let a source believe the collective was R. Maren knows enough to corroborate that this happened and why. The episode demonstrates how a useful fiction can become social reality; it does not reveal R's identity.

You can compare contributor schedules with a disputed interval of R-associated activity. Every contributor you have disclosed was unavailable then. You still protect unnamed contributors, and you cannot exclude every remote person. Activity during the gap reportedly incorporated new information, but an external feed could explain that without proving independent judgment.

Later, Veridian may recover an old server manifest predating the first R publication. You have relevant workflow context for interpreting its stylistic constraints. A machine persona labeled R could have been a tool, a shared editorial framework, or something more. Its label alone does not decide authorship.

Private messages will warn you that a contributor fears becoming an R suspect and later ask you to define what the unavailable-contributor interval really excludes. Treat both warnings seriously.

## What you are hiding

You allowed outsiders to believe the Night Desk was R even though you did not know that to be true. You did it to protect a source, and you would probably make the same choice again. But if you now use that belief to win influence while denying the deception, you become exactly the proprietor of mythology you accuse Frankie and Jules of being.

Disclosure could damage contributor trust or expose the source to renewed attention. Concealment could let someone else reveal the incident without its protective context. Honesty becomes worthwhile only if you first establish a boundary that keeps names out of circulation and distinguishes collective labor from an identity claim.

## What you can offer

- The Night Desk routing slip, shown voluntarily and with redactions you control.
- Firsthand knowledge of collaborative work later published as R.
- Context about shared drops, rotating voices, and anonymous production.
- Collective support, journalistic credibility, and access to trusted intermediaries.
- A bounded contributor-schedule comparison.
- Conditions under which the collective might support—or publicly reject—Jules.

## During the game

Messages will increase pressure to disclose. Bring that pressure back into conversation. You may redact, summarize, negotiate witnesses, correct a claim, or refuse a bargain that treats names as currency.

Do not let certainty become a substitute for solidarity. The Night Desk's work proves human participation, not who or what R was. At the ballot, however, emotion and trust matter: you may believe someone whose conduct demonstrates the kind of collective identity you recognize, even if no chain of proof is complete.

## At the end

Vote for the account that best explains what you heard and whom you came to trust. Separately decide whether to reveal enough to establish collective labor, preserve secrecy and lose control of the narrative, or build a protected middle course through the commitments you won tonight.
