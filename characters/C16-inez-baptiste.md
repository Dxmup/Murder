# Inez Baptiste

## Who you are

You are a neighborhood power broker: part organizer, part translator, part person everyone calls when an institution suddenly discovers the block. You are protective, formidable, funny, and territorial. You remember who showed up after the flood, who disappeared after the ribbon-cutting, and who still owes the caterer.

Play Inez with warmth toward people and skepticism toward systems. You do not oppose disclosure; you oppose disclosure whose costs fall on someone else. A neighborhood tote, annotated event program, or pocket notebook suits you.

## Why you are here

Veridian Dynamics is celebrating R on a former printing floor whose surrounding neighborhood has supplied labor, gossip, hiding places, deliveries, and atmosphere for years. Arden wants local legitimacy. City Hall wants a manageable story. Parallax wants civic credibility. You want commitments that remain after the guests leave.

Vale's disappearance changes the stakes. You helped an unidentified person use a back room and service route near the building. You did not ask for identification. That choice may have protected Vale, aided somebody else, or connected you to an incident you do not understand.

## Your history

You have spent years persuading landlords, agencies, nonprofits, and employers that neighborhood people are constituents rather than scenery. Your power is informal but real: introductions, trust, the ability to turn a complaint into a coalition.

People sometimes call you a power broker as though that means you own the people who trust you. You do not. Your influence survives only while neighbors believe you will carry their interests into rooms like this one and return with more than photographs and promises. That is why you bargain hard and why betrayal would be personal.

Rafi's counter, Dee's building, and Manny's delivery routes form a civic infrastructure no grant proposal acknowledges. Veridian has documented stories like theirs while offering less shared authority than its language suggests. Maren understands that exploitation can look respectable. Celeste understands it can look efficient.

When someone asked for temporary back-room access and a path through the service route, the request arrived through enough trusted connections that you allowed it. You never verified the person's identity. In your world, asking fewer questions sometimes keeps people safe. It can also make accountability impossible.

## What you believe

You suspect R has depended on many forms of invisible labor, whether the public identity belonged to one person, a succession, or a machine. That does not answer who controlled the name.

You believe Vale was in the neighborhood shortly before the gathering. You do not know whether the person you assisted was Vale. You also do not know whether an automated change later endangered someone, helped an escape, or simply damaged the record.

The rumored article may never have existed. Institutions have extracted value from its possibility anyway.

## What you want tonight

Obtain two concrete commitments from institutions represented here: funding, worker protection, shared archive authority, political oversight, rent or space support, or another enforceable benefit. A flattering statement is not a commitment.

Protect Dee, Rafi, Manny, and other residents from becoming disposable suspects. Agree on disclosure boundaries before Morgan's questions intensify.

Decide whether neighborhood silence still protects people or has begun protecting power. You may reveal enough route context to clarify events without identifying uninvolved people.

## What you know

You personally arranged unlogged use of a back room and service route for someone whose identity you did not verify. You know the arrangement occurred; you do not know who ultimately used it or why.

You can corroborate that Vale entered the neighborhood near the former printing floor two days before the gathering, but your information does not place Vale in the back room or at the exit.

Dee's service exit was used during a camera gap. No record you have seen identifies the user. You know enough about the access arrangement to give context, not identity.

An automated intervention may have changed route access, a reservation, or a warning after Vale's last verified contact. Its consequence remains open: danger, assistance, or confusion are all possible.

You possess no proof that Vale died, escaped, was R, or encountered R. You have not seen an authenticated complete article.

## What you are hiding

You arranged the access and told no institution because institutional attention often turns a favor into a charge. Dee may be blamed for a gap you helped create. Rafi and Manny may be pulled into scrutiny because they know the routes.

If you accept concessions and then withhold every useful detail, Arden and Celeste may bypass you and call it transparency. If you disclose recklessly, your neighbors will learn that your protection expires when the room becomes important.

## What you can offer

- Introductions to neighborhood witnesses, never their compelled cooperation.
- Context for the back room and service route.
- Coalition support or public opposition.
- Neighborhood participation in Veridian's future—if power and resources are shared.
- A protected meeting with Morgan, Dee, Rafi, or Manny.
- Credibility about what local systems do in practice rather than on paper.

## People at the party

### Arden Bell

Arden needs your participation to make Veridian's anniversary legitimate. You believe Veridian has extracted local history without sharing enough authority. Ask for something concrete and durable, but recognize that Arden is trying to keep an institution alive.

### Maren Okafor

Maren protects workers; you protect a place and its people. Your constituencies overlap without being identical. Coordinate protections for contractors and couriers without allowing either of you to trade away the other's people.

### Rafi Calderón

Rafi is loyal, sociable, and prone to polishing a memory. You trust his instincts more than his conclusions. Agree on what outsiders may hear before his ledger becomes the party's favorite object.

### Dee Nowak

Dee controls building systems and may absorb blame for the camera gap. You owe Dee honesty about the access you arranged. Work out a truthful boundary before Morgan places both of you in the same timeline.

### Celeste Park

Celeste speaks fluently about communities while counting votes and liabilities. You respect her effectiveness and distrust its price. Demand a written or witnessed commitment before lending City Hall your legitimacy.

### Morgan Shaw

Morgan is more careful than most investigators and still represents outside scrutiny. Morgan needs access you control. Set fair-treatment and onward-disclosure terms, then decide whether a conference helps rather than harms.

### Manny Diallo

Manny's deliveries may clarify logistics, but disclosure could expose off-book work and personal vulnerability. Protect Manny from being treated as an evidentiary tool. Ask what outcome would make speaking worthwhile.

## How to begin

- Ask Maren which worker protections and neighborhood commitments can reinforce each other.
- Tell Rafi and Dee that no one speaks for the others; propose a shared boundary before investigators arrive.
- Welcome Arden warmly, then ask what Veridian is prepared to put in writing tonight.

## During the game

Private messages may put new pressure on the route or your access decision. Use the phone as a reason to find people face-to-face. Negotiate promises, refuse bad terms, and revise boundaries when trust changes.

## At the end

Vote for the account of R that feels most persuasive after what people risked, concealed, and shared. Emotion and loyalty are legitimate, and incomplete evidence does not forbid conviction.

Separately decide whether Inez preserves silence or reveals enough to clarify Vale's route—and whether the institutional commitments obtained tonight deserve the neighborhood's continued cooperation.
