# Arden Bell

## Who you are

You are the Executive Director of Veridian Dynamics, a nonprofit devoted to investigative journalism, public-interest technology, and the increasingly difficult proposition that either can survive without being owned by someone wealthy.

You are immaculate because disorder frightens donors. You are hospitable because hospitality is how institutions disguise triage. You remember names, notice empty glasses, and can make a request sound like an honor. Tonight, however, you are tired enough that the seams may show.

Play Arden as composed, observant, and constantly calculating—not cold, but practiced at postponing personal feelings until after the emergency. A polished jacket, event badge, clipboard, or elegant notebook would suit you.

## Why you are here

You are hosting *Fifteen Years of R* on the former printing floor of a defunct downtown newspaper. The anniversary was supposed to do three things at once: celebrate reporting published under the mysterious R byline, demonstrate Veridian’s cultural importance, and raise enough money to keep the organization alive.

Vale was expected to attend. An account associated with R promised the evening would include “a reckoning.” Then Vale disappeared.

You do not know who sent that message, what “a reckoning” means, or whether Vale ever possessed the devastating article everyone is now whispering about. Nevertheless, you allowed the implication of revelation to make the invitation irresistible. Without that implication, several people in this room—and much of the money you need—would not be here.

## Your history

You built your career persuading people with incompatible interests to fund the same public good. Journalists saw you as a fundraiser who understood editorial independence. Technologists saw you as a translator who could make civic responsibility legible to boards and investors. Neighborhood organizations were less impressed; they had seen institutions celebrate local stories while treating local people as scenery.

Veridian now occupies an uncomfortable middle ground. It safeguards neglected archives, supports investigations no commercial newsroom will fund, and hosts civic-technology projects. It also survives quarter to quarter. Payroll is approaching. A building obligation is overdue. Two expected sponsors quietly withdrew after rumors began circulating that R—or Vale, or both—had prepared an investigation of Parallax Systems.

Parallax is Veridian’s largest recent donor. Its founder, Dr. Mira Sen, offered money when few others would. The agreement, however, contains restrictions you did not fully disclose to your board. One provision allows funding to pause if Veridian presents unauthenticated technical claims as fact. You told yourself this was ordinary accuracy language. You also knew it could become leverage the moment Parallax itself became the subject of a claim.

You accepted the money because without it programs would close and people would lose work. You concealed the details because you believed the board might reject the gift on principle. Both decisions felt responsible at the time. Tonight they may look like corruption.

## What you believe

You do not have a settled answer about R. You have spent enough time around institutions to know that a single public identity can conceal an editor, a team, a donor, a tool, or an entire mythology. The longevity of the R voice impresses you, but continuity can be cultivated.

Your instinct is that several people and systems have contributed to R over the years. That does not tell you whether one person remained in control, whether the name passed between people, or whether machinery preceded all of them. You are suspicious of anyone offering a clean answer—especially when that answer increases their importance.

You also do not know whether the rumored article existed. An R-associated account referred to “the piece,” but no authenticated document accompanied the phrase. You let others imagine more because the ambiguity helped the event. That is not the same as inventing the rumor, although some guests may not appreciate the distinction.

## What you want tonight

Your first priority is keeping Veridian alive without openly surrendering its mission. Before the evening ends, obtain at least two different forms of meaningful support: money, political protection, neighborhood participation, labor cooperation, archive partnership, or a public commitment that makes another commitment possible.

You also need to make the anniversary legitimate. Frankie Lowell can give it journalistic lineage, but Frankie suspects Veridian is monetizing R. Inez Baptiste can give it neighborhood legitimacy, but Inez believes organizations like yours extract stories and leave obligations behind. Winning only one of them will make the other’s criticism sharper.

Finally, decide what to do about the Parallax restrictions. You may defend the agreement, renegotiate it with Mira, seek political cover from Celeste, disclose it and risk the institution, or find another course entirely. What matters is that you make a choice instead of allowing the restriction to govern Veridian invisibly.

## What you know

Veridian holds an intake report concerning the First R Letter, a paper letter Frankie associates with the beginning of the R byline. The report says the paper, ink, and fold pattern are consistent with the relevant period. It does not establish who wrote or delivered the letter.

The archive also contains an expense record that conflicts with Frankie’s famous account of receiving the letter in person. The discrepancy does not prove Frankie lied. Memory, bookkeeping, travel, and later retelling could explain it. But the record prevents you from endorsing the story as established fact.

The anniversary was partly justified by a message from an R-associated account promising “a reckoning.” The account association is real; the sender is unknown. Another R-associated communication referred to “the piece” without an attached or authenticated payload. You have never seen a complete article.

You know who funded the event, who requested invitations, and which guests arrived because they expected access to one another. You do not know who first proposed celebrating this exact anniversary. The suggestion seemed to emerge through several conversations until everyone remembered it as a plan already underway.

Later tonight, the archive may complete recovery of an older server manifest. Until you receive that message, you do not know what it contains.

## What you are hiding

You concealed the Parallax restrictions from Veridian’s board and continued presenting the donation as ordinary unrestricted support. If this becomes public, Frankie may call the anniversary purchased history, Maren may see workers used to launder corporate reputation, and Inez may conclude that Veridian’s promises are worthless.

Mira may argue that you understood the agreement perfectly. Celeste may know enough to make denial impossible. Graham may treat the restriction as market-moving evidence even though it proves nothing about R or Vale.

You are not required to confess. You may disclose selectively, negotiate first, or defend your decision. But concealment becomes harder each time you ask someone else to trust Veridian.

## What you can offer

- Controlled access to Veridian’s archives and provenance records.
- A place on the public program and the legitimacy that comes with it.
- Introductions among donors, journalists, officials, and community leaders.
- Information about event funding, invitations, and institutional reactions to the article rumor.
- Public credit, attribution, or correction.
- Future partnerships and material commitments—if you are willing to make them concrete.

You do not possess the First R Letter itself; Frankie controls the letter packet. You control the separate intake and expense records needed to assess Frankie’s story.

## People at the party

### Frankie Lowell

Frankie is the evening’s honored journalism legend and its most dangerous storyteller. You admire what Frankie built and resent the assumption that institutions survive on charm alone. Frankie needs your archive; you need Frankie’s blessing. Offer access only if you can agree on what the records do—and do not—establish.

### Dr. Mira Sen

Mira rescued programs that might otherwise have closed. Mira also negotiated the donation from a position of overwhelming power. You have been cordial enough to make outsiders think you are allies. Privately, each of you needs the other to accept responsibility for the agreement without controlling its meaning.

### Celeste Park

Celeste can make permits, introductions, and political hazards move. You respect that skill because it resembles your own. Celeste knows the donation and the event are vulnerable, and may offer protection that creates a new debt. Find out what City Hall feared before deciding how much to trust her.

### Graham Pike

Graham can write a check, frighten other donors, or turn one careless sentence into a market event. You invited Graham because excluding a loud financial actor does not make the actor disappear. Contain the spectacle, extract something useful, or establish publicly that Veridian will not validate claims for money.

### Inez Baptiste

Inez represents people who live and work around the building while institutions cycle through it. Inez believes Veridian wants neighborhood authenticity without neighborhood power. You need participation, not decorative approval. Be prepared to offer something that remains valuable after tonight.

### Maren Okafor

Maren distrusts mysteries that turn invisible labor into atmosphere. Veridian has praised worker-centered journalism without always funding it adequately. Maren can help make the evening morally serious, but only if your support extends beyond public language.

## How to begin

- Welcome Frankie personally. Say that the archive report is ready, but ask Frankie what form of public acknowledgment would feel honest before offering access.
- Find Inez early. Ask what Veridian would have to promise for neighborhood participation to be meaningful rather than extractive.
- Quietly assess Mira and Celeste. Do not reveal the donation problem immediately; learn first whether either already expects the restriction to be used tonight.

## During the game

Private messages will bring new pressure and new archive material. Treat them as reasons to return to the room, not as solutions. You may show records, quote them, summarize them selectively, or negotiate conditions around disclosure. Other players may distrust those conditions.

You are the event host, not the game’s judge. You cannot compel testimony, seize objects, authenticate R, or decide what happened to Vale. Your authority is social and institutional, and players are free to refuse you.

## At the end

Vote for the account you find most persuasive, even if the evidence is incomplete. Your judgment may be shaped by trust, betrayal, fear for Veridian, or admiration for someone’s reconstruction. You do not owe the room an archivist’s neutrality.

Separately, decide what Arden will do when morning comes: preserve Veridian through compromise, disclose the funding arrangement and risk collapse, or attempt a third course created through tonight’s bargains. That choice is yours regardless of who or what you believe R was.
