# Graham Pike

## Who you are

You are an activist short-seller: you research celebrated companies, bet that their value will fall, and publish reasons the market should agree. Admirers call you accountability with a Bloomberg terminal. Critics call you an arsonist who buys insurance first. Both descriptions flatter you.

Play Graham as combustible, funny, shamelessly transactional, and quick enough to make outrage entertaining. You enjoy saying the impolite financial truth, especially when everyone else is disguising money as principle. Under the performance is real discipline: a claim that cannot survive provenance scrutiny can ruin you. A loud pocket square, immaculate casualwear, annotated market chart, or expensive pen would fit.

## Why you are here

You have a position against Parallax Systems. A tip claimed R had prepared a market-moving Parallax investigation, and you amplified it. The rumor is now moving money even though nobody has produced a complete article.

The anniversary gives you a room full of possible corroborators, victims, deniers, and useful reactions. You want the R/AI story to affect Parallax's valuation without being exposed as its manufacturer. That means making people care while keeping the distance between amplification and invention visible.

## Your history

You learned finance by watching grand narratives outrun ordinary facts. New York rewards a visionary founder until a payroll spreadsheet becomes more persuasive. Your best campaigns revealed real misconduct that polite institutions had ignored. Your worst calls began with something true and grew more certain each time it passed through a person who needed it.

Parallax interests you because it sits where money, civic virtue, labor, and technical opacity overlap. Mira presents the company as public-interest infrastructure. Maren knows the workers hidden inside that infrastructure. Eli knows which technical descriptions are honest. Arden accepted Parallax money. Celeste anticipated political damage. All of that is material. None of it proves an R article.

You received a tip saying R had a devastating investigation. You repeated the claim with enough confidence that others treated your confidence as corroboration. You may have added context, sharpened language, or allowed a chain of repetitions to look like multiple sources.

## What you believe

You believe markets reveal what people fear, not necessarily what is true. Parallax's reaction and the city's concern suggest vulnerability. They do not establish the article.

Your working theory is that R is some kind of distributed or synthetic operation because the rumor propagated like a system rather than a scoop. That may be analysis contaminated by your position. A lone human could also seed a rumor strategically; a collective could run a pressure campaign; nobody may have intended the complete story at all.

You are willing to vote from pattern and instinct. You are not willing to pretend you saw a document you did not see—unless the pressure and profit become too attractive.

## What you want tonight

Induce two people outside finance to treat the R/Parallax rumor as materially consequential, then survive a public challenge about where it came from.

Acquire bounded technical context from Eli or Mira without buying the wording. A qualified statement is still valuable if you can explain why. A purchased declaration of certainty will collapse under scrutiny.

Control the story of the first tip. Persuade Arden, Celeste, Kit, or another credible listener that you amplified analysis rather than fabricated a source—or decide to admit how aggressively you improved the rumor.

## What you know

You personally received and amplified a tip claiming R had a market-moving Parallax investigation. You know the message or record you received and the changes you made when repeating it. You do not know whether the original source was Vale, a human R, a group, a system, a market manipulator, or ordinary rumor.

You know no source has produced a complete authenticated article. Market movement proves belief and exposure, not a manuscript.

You have descriptions of two alleged fragments that conflict in subject, format, or provenance. One or both could be drafts, fakes, generated bait, unrelated writing, or partial work from different people. Their existence does not prove a complete article.

Parallax and City Hall planned for the possibility of an R story without recording that either Mira or Celeste had seen it. Their reaction is financially meaningful. It is not authentication.

You may receive an offer to cover your position if you obtain a technical source willing to say R operated autonomously. The buyer may not care when that alleged autonomy began. That timing omission could turn a narrow technical observation into a false history.

## What you are hiding

You may have seeded the first public form of the article rumor—or amplified a private tip so effectively that the distinction is academic. Your financial exposure increases every time another person calls the rumor confirmed, and the same amplification that makes you money can make market manipulation look more credible than the article.

You are tempted to pay for certainty: settle Rafi's debt for a dramatic ledger interpretation, buy Morgan's conclusion, or reward Eli for a sentence stripped of caveats. Doing so may move the market tonight and destroy you later.

Honesty becomes worthwhile if someone gives you a defensible fact, if disclosure protects your larger campaign, or if you decide accountability matters more than winning this particular trade.

## What you can offer

- Money, debt settlement, research help, and market access.
- Your original tip record and an honest account of how it spread.
- Descriptions of the conflicting alleged fragments.
- A platform capable of making a disclosure consequential.
- Financial analysis of who benefited from the rumor.
- Public acknowledgment of your position when testing other people's claims.

## People at the party

### Dr. Mira Sen

Mira believes you turn uncertainty into sabotage; you believe Mira turns control into public service. Your interests are openly opposed, which can make the conversation unusually honest. Seek a bounded reaction or document, not a theatrical denial you can misquote.

### Eli Navarro

Eli has the technical credibility your trade lacks. You can fund protection or legal help, but Eli expects you to weaponize every caveat. Offer terms that leave Eli in control of the exact wording, then decide whether you can live with the answer.

### Arden Bell

Arden wants your money and fears your spectacle. You need Veridian's legitimacy. A pledge could look like capture; a public fight could destroy the fundraiser. Bargain over transparency rather than pretending either of you is above leverage.

### Celeste Park

Celeste wants market calm, and you want government reaction because reaction moves markets. Celeste knows how far the rumor traveled. You can expose political concern; Celeste can frame you as the source of the crisis.

### Rafi Calderón

Rafi has a ledger pattern the market would love and a debt you could easily settle. Do not mistake purchasing access for purchasing a conclusion. Rafi may sell you a splendid story and later admit several incompatible collectors.

### Kit Rakes

Kit brings audience, energy, and a complete theory assembled from facts and leaps. You amplify each other while privately doubting each other. Fund the investigation, exploit the performance, or publicly demand corrections; any choice reveals what kind of truth-teller you are.

### Morgan Shaw

Morgan needs payment and holds a partial Vale timeline. You want a conclusion. Offer money for work, not certainty. If Morgan refuses your preferred answer, that refusal may be more credible than anything you could buy.

## How to begin

- Tell Arden exactly what kind of public reaction would justify a pledge, and disclose that you have a financial position.
- Ask Kit to identify which part of the grand theory rests on evidence rather than repetition; offer reach in exchange for visible corrections.
- Approach Rafi with two separate offers: one for settling the debt, another for bounded ledger access. Do not make forgiveness conditional on a conclusion.

## During the game

Messages may show the rumor moving or tempt you to buy an autonomous-operation claim. Let greed, fear, rivalry, and admiration influence you; this is a human game. But other players may challenge provenance, and a claim does not become evidence because it moved a price.

You control your records and money. Nobody must sell, cooperate, or adopt your interpretation. The most powerful move available to Graham may be admitting uncertainty before someone else exposes it.

## At the end

Vote for the R account you find persuasive. Separately decide whether to admit a market tactic and accept the cost, or escalate a rumor that may be false. State whether Graham's campaign is about accountability or simply winning—and accept that the room may judge the answer.
