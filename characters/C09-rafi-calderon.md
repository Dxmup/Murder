# Rafi Calderón

## Who you are

You own the all-night bodega and package counter nearest the building woven through the R stories. You know who buys coffee at 4:00 a.m., whose packages arrive under a cousin's name, and which delivery workers need ten quiet minutes out of the rain. You remember faces well and stories even better—occasionally better than they happened.

Play Rafi as sociable, practical, proud, and hard to rush. You enjoy a good audience, but you are not comic relief or a quaint neighborhood oracle. Outsiders routinely discover that your ordinary records are indispensable, then act as though that entitles them to the people inside them. An apron, receipt book, reading glasses, or roll of adding-machine paper would suit you.

## Why you are here

Veridian invited you as a witness to the neighborhood history of R. You heard something else: an opportunity to settle an old unpaid account and decide who gets to tell a story your shop helped sustain.

For years, an R-associated account paid for packages, messenger fees, prepaid phones, coffee, and small conveniences. The balance was never fully settled. Several people collected items. Some instructions arrived automatically. Everyone now wants the ledger, but very few have asked what disclosure might cost your customers.

## Your history

Your shop survived rent spikes, blackouts, app delivery, and three different waves of people announcing they had “discovered” the block. You extended credit because formal systems did not. You accepted packages because apartment doors were unreliable. You learned discretion because discretion kept neighbors safe.

R-associated transactions developed a peculiar habit: a non-public phrase, preference, or ritual repeated across communications and purchases. You noticed it long before anyone paid you to notice. It felt like continuity. Later, it shifted subtly. The timing is hard to pin down, and the collectors around that account were not one consistent person.

You have told the story as if you knew R. What you really knew was an account, a routine, some collectors, and a pattern. That is still knowledge. It is simply less cinematic.

## What you believe

Your heart favors a human explanation because habits feel personal. You have watched people preserve tiny rituals across decades. But shopkeeping has also taught you that families share accounts, workers inherit routines, and apps repeat preferences more faithfully than people do.

The ledger supports several interpretations. One hidden person may have used couriers. A group may have maintained a protocol. A persistent persona setting may have generated the habit automatically. The later change could be age, a handoff, or an update. You will listen, though you dislike being lectured about your own counter.

## What you want tonight

- **Primary Goal:** Settle old obligations tied to R-associated transactions without letting outsiders own the neighborhood story.
  - *Success Condition:* Resolve the outstanding account through payment, a credible promise, or a deliberate public forgiveness and have one neighbor support the choice.
- **Secondary Goal 1 (Social & Supportive):** Make the ledger valuable without overclaiming it.
  - *Success Condition:* Trade a bounded transaction pattern to Morgan, Graham, or Frankie for something concrete.
- **Secondary Goal 2 (Social & Supportive):** Protect local customers and side arrangements.
  - *Success Condition:* Reach a mutual disclosure boundary with Dee, Inez, or Manny.
- **Your Final Choice:** Monetize continuity claims or admit the ledger records several incompatible customers.

## What you know

You personally observed the recurring private habit across R-associated transactions and communications. It was not public trivia when you noticed it. You cannot say whether one person, several people, or a system maintained it.

Your account strip shows transaction continuity, several collectors, and a disputed alteration in the pattern. It does not identify a customer as R, establish residence or authorship, or give a precise date for any transfer of control.

The collectors contradicted one another in appearance, schedule, and demeanor. Some may have been ordinary couriers. Some payments or pickup instructions arrived through automated systems. Neither fact eliminates a single hidden customer.

Morgan believes your records may independently help date the subtle change. Morgan has promised not to call any customer R. That is a useful promise, not yet sufficient protection.

## What you are hiding

You sometimes accepted cash and ignored identification rules. Once, as a favor, you altered a ledger entry. The favor was unrelated to establishing R's identity, but disclosure could expose neighbors, threaten your license, and make the entire ledger look fabricated.

You have also polished the “one loyal customer” version of the story because people listen to it. In truth, multiple incompatible collectors used the account. If exposed after you sell certainty, Inez and Dee may withdraw their protection and outsiders may treat every neighborhood witness as dishonest.

Honesty becomes worthwhile if the debt is settled fairly, attribution stays bounded, and the people behind the transactions are protected rather than mined for content.

## What you can offer

- The account strip and a longer private ledger you control.
- Exact context for payments, package holds, collectors, and pickup routines.
- Introductions to neighborhood witnesses who will not speak to strangers without your word.
- A plausible continuity story—and the contradictions that weaken it.
- Forgiveness of the old balance, which would be a meaningful public choice rather than surrender.

## People at the party

### Dee Nowak

You and Dee have covered small emergencies for one another for years. Your timing and collector accounts conflict, partly because shift changes created blind spots. Align the bounded facts without manufacturing certainty. Dee needs your context; you need Dee's protection if the ledger alteration surfaces.

### Inez Baptiste

Inez is the person you trust to understand that silence can be community care rather than conspiracy. You disagree about outsiders: you are willing to make a deal; Inez wants enforceable benefits first. Ask Inez to support whatever you decide about the debt.

### Morgan Shaw

Morgan is the only formal investigator here and at least knows how to say “I don't know.” Morgan wants a bounded ledger view, not your conclusion. Negotiate attribution, protection, and payment before cooperating. Then hold Morgan to the limits.

### Graham Pike

Graham will pay for a dramatic reading and call it market intelligence. The money could settle the account or endanger the shop. If you trade information, make Graham repeat exactly what the ledger cannot prove.

### Frankie Lowell

Frankie loves a stable human habit because it makes R feel like a person one could have discovered. You enjoy Frankie and recognize a fellow embellisher. Compare the private habit with Frankie's memory, but do not let two good stories become authentication.

### Manny Diallo, if present

Manny collected and delivered items through your counter and is still owed money for some work. Each of you holds half of several transactions. Affection and frustration coexist. Settle what you can without exposing Manny's vulnerabilities.

## How to begin

- Find Inez or Dee and agree on what neighborhood details are off limits until protections exist.
- Ask Frankie to describe R's supposed private habit before showing that your ledger contains something similar.
- Tell Morgan you will discuss the pattern, but only after Morgan offers clear terms on attribution and customer privacy.

## During the game

Messages may increase pressure to settle the account or date the pattern's change. They do not make the account holder R. Use the room: ask who wants the ledger, what they offer, and what they plan to say afterward.

You may exaggerate socially, but do not invent evidence. Correct yourself if a bargain or relationship makes honesty worthwhile. Your loyalty to neighbors may matter more than being seen as the person who knew R.

## At the end

Vote for the account of R you believe after hearing the room. Emotion and lived judgment count alongside records. Separately decide whether to monetize the most valuable continuity story or admit publicly that the ledger records several incompatible customers. You may also forgive the debt—but make that a choice, not something extracted from you.
