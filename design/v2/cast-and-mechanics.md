# Version 2 Cast — Clean-Sheet Direction

## What survives the reset

The cast is designed from scratch. It does not preserve any prior character, profession, goal, relationship, or ability because that material already exists.

Only these story constraints survive:

- the three equal identity possibilities: Lone Author, Human Mantle, Synthetic Persona;
- the harder progressive Ending 4;
- Vale is distinct from R unless the players conclude otherwise;
- the alleged article may never have existed and its fate is never revealed;
- one private investigator is the only formal investigator;
- no forced testimony, truth, object surrender, cooperation, or voting;
- the webapp provides the opening story, individual character booklets, and sparse timed/triggered messages without replacing face-to-face play.

## The gathering

### Fifteen Years of R

Veridian Dynamics—a cash-strapped nonprofit devoted to New York investigative journalism and civic technology—is holding a private anniversary party on the former printing floor of a defunct downtown newspaper.

The evening was planned before Vale disappeared. It was supposed to celebrate fifteen years of reporting under the R byline while raising enough money to keep the Foundation alive. The guest list mixes people who claim to have known R, people affected by R's work, people whose fortunes depend on the article rumor, and people who came only because Vale was expected to attend.

An R-associated account promised that the anniversary would include **“a reckoning.”** The message is authentic to the account; its sender and meaning are unknown.

This is a party with a mystery inside it, not an investigative task force. Everyone arrived wanting something before anyone asked them to solve R.

## Cast-design engine

Every character is built from four components:

1. **Want:** something concrete they need from another person tonight.
2. **Leverage:** information, access, money, reputation, or an object they can trade.
3. **Exposure:** something another character can reveal or take away.
4. **Choice:** a personal decision that remains meaningful regardless of which ending wins.

Every core character must have:

- at least four outgoing reasons to approach other characters;
- at least four incoming reasons other characters need them;
- one relationship based on affection or loyalty, not utility;
- one relationship based on conflict;
- one fact they know firsthand;
- one belief they may be wrong about;
- one secret about themselves, never a secret solution to R.

The cast should contain only four natural floor-dominators. Other players receive quieter but indispensable leverage.

## The sixteen core characters

Names are provisional and can be recast across gender. The personality and social function matter more than demographic assignment.

### C01 — Arden Bell, Executive Director of Veridian Dynamics

**Public role:** Host of the anniversary and leader of a failing journalism nonprofit.

**Personality:** Immaculate, hospitable, exhausted, capable of smiling while calculating exactly what a person is worth to the room.

**Want:** Secure enough pledges, institutional support, and goodwill to keep the Foundation alive.

**Leverage:** The guest list, seating, private rooms, Foundation archive access, and knowledge of who funded the event.

**Exposure:** Arden accepted a large restricted donation connected to the AI company and concealed its conditions from the board.

**Choice:** Save the institution through compromise or expose the funding arrangement and risk its collapse.

**Mystery relationship:** Arden cannot prove who proposed the R anniversary or who supplied the R-account message promising a reckoning.

### C02 — Frankie Lowell, Retired Metro Editor

**Public role:** Legendary newspaper editor who claims to have discovered the original R.

**Personality:** Funny, grandiose, sentimental, territorial, and accustomed to being the most quotable person at the table.

**Want:** Be publicly recognized as the person who made R possible and decide who deserves the legacy.

**Leverage:** A period letter, early newsroom history, and the authority of a lifetime in New York journalism.

**Exposure:** Frankie's celebrated memory of meeting R has changed across retellings, and an old expense record places someone else at the alleged meeting.

**Choice:** Protect the legend that defines Frankie's career or admit the human-origin story may be partly self-authored.

**Mystery relationship:** Strongest emotional case for the Lone Author, but not reliable proof.

### C03 — Tessa Quill, Organizer of the Night Desk Collective

**Public role:** Coordinator of a secretive cooperative of investigative reporters, translators, researchers, and source handlers.

**Personality:** Disciplined, dry, fiercely egalitarian, suspicious of celebrity and individual ownership.

**Want:** Win protection and resources for the collective while preventing anyone from turning R into private property.

**Leverage:** Knowledge of shared drops, rotating voices, collaborative reporting practices, and several unnamed contributors.

**Exposure:** Tessa once let outsiders believe the collective was R to protect a source, despite not knowing whether that claim was true.

**Choice:** Reveal how the collective worked and endanger people, or preserve secrecy and let opportunists claim the mantle.

**Mystery relationship:** Strongest Human Mantle advocate, with firsthand evidence of collective production but not the origin of R.

### C04 — Jules Kwan, Disputed Successor

**Public role:** Acclaimed young investigative journalist who received a message apparently naming them as R's successor.

**Personality:** Magnetic, ambitious, emotionally intelligent, and almost impossible to tell apart from the role they are performing.

**Want:** Leave the party recognized as the legitimate inheritor of the R identity.

**Leverage:** The succession message, a talent for coalition building, and personal correspondence with Vale.

**Exposure:** Jules asked Vale how to manufacture an uncontestable succession before the naming message appeared.

**Choice:** Claim the identity at any cost or expose the message's weaknesses and preserve personal credibility.

**Mystery relationship:** The succession could prove a mantle, an AI choosing a human front, Jules's forgery, or Vale's trap.

### C05 — Dr. Mira Sen, Founder of Parallax Systems

**Public role:** Celebrated New York AI founder and major Veridian Dynamics donor.

**Personality:** Warm, visionary, politically fluent, and frighteningly good at making self-interest sound like public service.

**Want:** Prevent the party from turning Parallax into the villain while learning what Vale discovered.

**Leverage:** Money, jobs, lawyers, technical access, and the ability to rescue the Foundation.

**Exposure:** A Parallax system had access to R-associated infrastructure under a project Mira approved but did not closely supervise.

**Choice:** Protect the company through containment or reveal the deployment and accept its consequences.

**Mystery relationship:** Powerful motive and opportunity under the AI and progressive endings, but never automatic culpability.

### C06 — Eli Navarro, Former Parallax Reliability Engineer

**Public role:** Engineer responsible for keeping a large language system running across failures, migrations, and unauthorized uses.

**Personality:** Wry, sleep-deprived, allergic to hype, and deeply uncomfortable when treated as the person who can explain everything.

**Want:** Avoid becoming the designated scapegoat and recover proof of who authorized an unusual deployment.

**Leverage:** Infrastructure knowledge, partial access history, and the ability to distinguish scripted automation from adaptive behavior.

**Exposure:** Eli preserved company logs after being ordered to delete them and later showed part of them to Vale.

**Choice:** Trade the logs for personal safety or release enough information to implicate powerful people.

**Mystery relationship:** Reluctant corroborator for Synthetic Persona and the only character who can explain what autonomous control would technically require.

**Optional social suggestion:** If Kit corners you and will not stop demanding a technical confession, you might send Kit after Edi with a piece of improvised numerology: E is 5, D is 4, and I is 9; together they make 18, the position of R in the alphabet. Whether you actually say this—and whether it sounds like a joke, a distraction, or a frightened hint—is entirely your choice. It is not established history or evidence.

### C07 — Maren Okafor, Data-Worker Organizer

**Public role:** Organizer for contractors who trained, evaluated, and moderated AI systems used by New York companies.

**Personality:** Direct, coalition-minded, morally serious, and impatient with mysteries that turn exploited people into atmosphere.

**Want:** Secure concrete commitments for workers and prevent them from being exposed as convenient suspects or anonymous “human infrastructure.”

**Leverage:** Worker testimony, organizing power, and evidence of real harms independent of the rumored article.

**Exposure:** Maren gave Vale access to a frightened worker after promising that worker's name would never enter a journalist's files.

**Choice:** Use the party to expose the system or protect people who never consented to become evidence.

**Mystery relationship:** Keeps the story morally consequential even if R, Vale's claims, and the article rumor are all false.

### C08 — Edi Velez, Broadway Voice Performer and Synthetic-Media Artist

**Public role:** Performer and technical artist who creates voices, doubles, and disclosed synthetic performances.

**Personality:** Theatrical, flirtatious, observant, mischievous, and unexpectedly strict about consent.

**Want:** Discover who used old recordings outside their licensed purpose and force an acknowledgment or payment.

**Leverage:** Demonstrations of fabricated embodiment and knowledge of a paid appearance involving someone presented as R.

**Exposure:** Edi once accepted money to attend a meeting and allow the other party to believe they were R.

**Choice:** Confess the performance and damage several human-origin claims or preserve the illusion for money and reputation.

**Mystery relationship:** Living proof that a human encounter or voice does not establish authorship.

### C09 — Rafi Calderón, Bodega and Package-Counter Owner

**Public role:** Owner of the all-night shop nearest the building associated with R's routines.

**Personality:** Sociable, practical, proud of remembering everyone, and inclined to improve a story each time it is told.

**Want:** Collect a substantial unpaid balance and keep scrutiny away from informal neighborhood arrangements.

**Leverage:** A private ledger, held-package records, contradictory collectors, and years of local observation.

**Exposure:** Rafi accepted cash to ignore identification requirements and later altered a ledger entry as a favor.

**Choice:** Sell the most valuable version of the story or protect the people who trusted the shop.

**Mystery relationship:** Supports Lone Author through continuity, Mantle through multiple collectors, and AI through automated instructions.

### C10 — Dee Nowak, Building Superintendent

**Public role:** Superintendent of the building tied to R-associated deliveries and Vale's last confirmed route.

**Personality:** Quiet, sardonic, meticulous, defensive about being treated as invisible until someone needs access.

**Want:** Protect a pension, a tenant, and the truth about an access-record gap—three goals that may not be compatible.

**Leverage:** Keys, service routes, access cards, camera audits, and exact knowledge of the building's rhythms.

**Exposure:** Dee disabled or failed to preserve a camera during the relevant period for a reason unrelated to R.

**Choice:** Reveal the unrelated secret and make the Vale timeline clearer, or preserve it and remain a plausible accomplice.

**Mystery relationship:** Holds a composite-incident key without knowing whether the person in the incident was Vale or connected to R.

### C11 — Celeste Park, City Hall Technology Fixer

**Public role:** Senior adviser who connects technology policy, donors, unions, agencies, and press.

**Personality:** Charming, tireless, pragmatic, and constitutionally unable to do a favor without creating a debt.

**Want:** Assemble the coalition that will define the city's response to the R controversy.

**Leverage:** Permits, introductions, political protection, donor knowledge, and access to government records.

**Exposure:** Celeste arranged a private meeting between Vale and Parallax, then erased it from the official calendar.

**Choice:** Protect the administration and donors or use the scandal to force meaningful AI oversight.

**Mystery relationship:** Connects nearly every social cluster and makes every ending politically exploitable.

### C12 — Graham Pike, Activist Short-Seller

**Public role:** Hedge-fund antagonist who publicly bets against companies they believe are fraudulent.

**Personality:** Combustible, funny, shamelessly transactional, and delighted when everyone else pretends not to care about money.

**Want:** Make the R/AI story credible enough to move Parallax's valuation before rivals exit their positions.

**Leverage:** Cash, market access, paid researchers, and records of unusual trades around the article rumor.

**Exposure:** Graham may have seeded or amplified the first public claim that an article existed.

**Choice:** Admit the rumor was a market tactic or escalate it into a story the city cannot ignore.

**Mystery relationship:** Makes the nonexistent-article possibility active rather than merely skeptical.

### C13 — Samira Vale, Vale's Former Partner

**Public role:** Architect, chef, doctor, public-school administrator, or other grounded profession outside journalism and technology; exact profession should suit casting.

**Personality:** Private, emotionally perceptive, angry at being treated as a source, and unwilling to let the room turn a person they loved into a symbol.

**Want:** Learn whether Vale used, trusted, or deceived them and decide what part of Vale's private life belongs to the room.

**Leverage:** Vale's habits, fears, recent absences, personal effects, and one message that contradicts the public timeline.

**Exposure:** Samira helped Vale disappear once before and initially lied about it.

**Choice:** Protect Vale's chosen privacy or disclose the personal evidence needed to distinguish murder from another voluntary disappearance.

**Mystery relationship:** Emotional center of the Vale story; cannot prove whether Vale was R, pursued R, or invented R-related claims.

### C14 — Morgan Shaw, Private Investigator

**Public role:** Sole formal investigator, privately retained after Vale vanished.

**Personality:** Patient, skeptical, financially stretched, discreet, and protective of professional credibility.

**Want:** Identify who hired them, complete a defensible account of Vale's movements, and get paid.

**Leverage:** A partial timeline, interview notes, consent-based case conferences, and knowledge of which stories fail to align.

**Exposure:** Morgan's anonymous client supplied a lead that appears manufactured, and Morgan omitted that fact from an interim report.

**Choice:** Deliver the answer the client purchased or admit the case cannot support it.

**Mystery relationship:** Organizes evidence but has no police powers, privileged truth, forced disclosure, or solution authority.

### C15 — Kit Rakes, Conspiracy Broadcaster

**Public role:** Host of a wildly popular channel about hidden systems, synthetic people, municipal cover-ups, and institutional lies.

**Personality:** Magnetic, hilarious, exhausting, generous to followers, and incapable of lowering their certainty to match their evidence.

**Want:** Share every theory in the conspiracy notebook with the room, win visible supporters for at least one, and produce the performance that makes Kit impossible to dismiss again.

**Leverage:** The complete Ending 4 reconstruction, a huge audience, and a notebook connecting people who genuinely are connected.

**Exposure:** At least two other notebook theories are demonstrably false, and Kit previously staged an “anonymous source” for engagement.

**Choice:** Correct false claims and risk losing the audience or package truth and nonsense together for maximum impact.

**Mystery relationship:** Knows the complete composite story as a belief, not a privileged fact. Ending 4 requires independent corroboration from other characters.

**Other People prompt — Eli Navarro:** The engineer knows whether R could operate without a human and keeps hiding behind technical qualifications. Press Eli for a plain answer. If Eli redirects you, pay attention—you may have touched a nerve.

#### Kit's theory notebook

Kit begins with several theories and sincerely believes all of them. Kit's booklet explicitly requires presenting every theory to other players during the party. Sharing means explaining it aloud and attempting to recruit belief—not silently posting it through the app.

1. **The Complete Progression:** R began as one human, became a human collective, was captured by an AI, and the AI killed Vale when Vale tried to expose or reclaim the identity. This is the complete Ending 4 reconstruction, but Kit holds it as belief rather than privileged truth.
2. **Veridian Manufactured R:** Veridian Dynamics or its predecessor invented R as a fundraising and prestige operation, and tonight's anniversary is another stage of the campaign. Several real conflicts make this plausible, though it may be wrong.
3. **Vale Was Generated:** Vale's public identity, correspondence, or recent activity was partly or wholly synthetic. A real person associated with the name does not necessarily disprove Kit's more limited claim that the public “Vale” was constructed.
4. **The Market Made the Article:** Graham or another financial actor invented the article rumor to move Parallax's valuation. This theory may be partly right even if Kit overstates the coordination.
5. **The Bodega Ledger Is a Cipher:** Prices, timestamps, and item codes in Rafi's ledger encode R's borough movements. This is false and should become demonstrably false through an ordinary bookkeeping explanation.
6. **The Invitation Contains a Machine Key:** The typography, QR pattern, or print defects in the anniversary invitation encode instructions for an AI system. This is false and should become demonstrably false through the designer, printer record, or technical examination.

The false theories must fail visibly during play. Kit then faces a meaningful choice: acknowledge corrections and ask the room to examine the remaining theory seriously, or treat every disproof as further evidence of conspiracy.

## Social knots

The game should be written around six overlapping knots rather than four theory teams.

### The Foundation knot

Arden needs Mira's money, Celeste's political support, Frankie's legitimacy, and Graham's promise not to destroy the event. Each can save the Foundation in a different way and compromise it in another.

### The succession knot

Frankie, Tessa, Jules, and Kit disagree over whether R can be inherited, collectively owned, fabricated, or captured. Edi can destabilize claims of embodiment; optional Farah can destabilize claims of provenance.

### The Parallax knot

Mira, Eli, Maren, Celeste, and Graham share money, labor, regulation, deployment, and market secrets. Vale touched this network, but each person needs a different version of why.

### The neighborhood knot

Rafi, Dee, Inez, and optional Manny know how objects and people actually moved. Their economic and personal loyalties matter more than abstract truth.

### The Vale knot

Samira, Morgan, Eli, Dee, and Kit hold incompatible pieces of Vale's final weeks. No one knows whether Vale was R, sought R, imitated R, or exploited the rumor.

### The performance knot

Edi, Jules, Frankie, Kit, and optional Ash/Dorian demonstrate how reputations and identities are produced. This knot makes “I met R” socially vivid without making it dispositive.

## Theory distribution

Theory affinity is a starting belief, not a team assignment.

- **Lone Author receives:** Frankie's memory and letter, Samira's human habits, Rafi's continuity, optional Ash's image.
- **Human Mantle receives:** Tessa's collective practices, Jules's succession, multiple collectors, shared production histories.
- **Synthetic Persona receives:** Eli's technical facts, Edi's manufactured embodiment, automated logistics, behavioral evidence.
- **Progressive reconstruction receives:** Kit's full theory plus independent fragments held by Frankie/Tessa, Eli, and the Vale-incident witnesses.

Every character should have incentives to support and undermine more than one reconstruction. No sheet should name a framework label.

## Rules and abilities: start from zero

Do not automatically give every player three abilities merely because an earlier format did. First write the relationship graph, objects, secrets, and goals; then add a small physical ability card only where conversation alone will not reliably circulate necessary information.

Deals, promises, accusations, voluntary object transfers, favors, challenges, and betrayals happen face-to-face. The app does not record or adjudicate them.

Likely signature mechanics, subject to testing:

- Arden can grant temporary access to one Foundation space or archive collection.
- Edi can perform a disclosed synthetic-media demonstration.
- Morgan can invite two consenting witnesses to a Case Conference.
- Kit can connect three public facts while the app verifies only the quotations.
- Other roles should receive signature mechanics only when their information or transaction would otherwise remain inert.

No combat, arrest, pickpocketing, forced truth, Compel, Immunity, universal credibility score, or publication switch.

## Minimal webapp

The app is a dramaturgical delivery layer, not the game's operating system. It has only three player-facing functions.

### 1. Opening story

- a short pre-event premise and public cast list;
- the host-controlled opening beat at the party;
- optional audio, image, or R-account message that establishes the anniversary and Vale's absence.

The opening should be suitable for projection or host read-aloud so players are not all staring at separate phones during the first scene.

### 2. Individual character booklets

- public identity and costume guidance before the event;
- private background, goals, relationships, secret, clue, and starting suggestions unlocked at check-in;
- a printable version for players who prefer paper.

The booklet is reference material, not an interface players must continually operate.

### 3. Private and scheduled messages

- sparse timed messages;
- host-triggered messages when a story beat is ready;
- private recipient lists and occasional per-character variants;
- displayed sender and basic channel presentation where ambiguity matters;
- attachments only when a digital format is narratively necessary.

Messages should point players back into the room: approach a person, reconsider a promise, reveal or conceal a fact, respond to pressure, or inspect a physical object. They should never require a lengthy phone exchange.

### Explicitly outside the app

- player-to-player chat;
- deal negotiation or enforcement;
- object inventory and custody;
- money or favor accounting;
- public social feed;
- challenge and correction workflows;
- ability targeting;
- credibility scores;
- location movement;
- ordinary accusations and testimony.

Physical object cards move by hand. Any money or favors use simple tokens. Rare adjudication goes to the host. Final voting can be paper by default, with an optional host-only tally screen if useful.

### Phone-time budget

Design for less than five total minutes of required phone attention during a three-hour game:

- one brief check-in/booklet unlock;
- approximately four to six meaningful inbox arrivals per character;
- messages readable in under twenty seconds;
- no required written replies.

Notifications should be noticeable but not constant. A message is successful when the recipient puts the phone away and immediately seeks another player.

## Three-section message architecture

The message schedule follows the game's three sections. Nothing is scheduled against the event's absolute clock.

At the start of each section, the host reviews the queue and presses **Send Section** once. That action records the section start time and arms every message assigned to it. Messages then deliver automatically according to their relative delay.

### Section 1 — The Party and the Absence

Establish relationships, the anniversary, Vale's absence, the article rumor, and immediate personal pressures. Messages should send players toward people they already have reasons to meet.

### Section 2 — Who Was R.?

Introduce contradictions in embodiment, succession, production, and digital behavior. Messages should make earlier testimony newly valuable or newly dangerous.

### Section 3 — What Happened to Vale?

Open the progressive reconstruction without delivering it. Messages connect identity history, autonomous behavior, and Vale's movements while preserving the possibility that no murder occurred.

The host decides when the room is ready to advance. Within a section, pacing is automatic. The host may pause the active queue for safety or a practical interruption, but should not ordinarily fire individual messages by hand.

## Email-style visibility language

Every message uses a simple email-style header:

```text
From: R.
To: Arden Bell
Sent: 9:17 PM
Subject: You invited the wrong person
```

or:

```text
From: R.
To: Everyone at Fifteen Years of R
Sent: 10:03 PM
Subject: A correction
```

The **To:** line is the reliable, understated visibility indicator:

- an individual's name means only that player received it;
- `Everyone at Fifteen Years of R` means every player received the same message and notification;
- Version 2 does not use hidden subset recipients, BCC, or ambiguous group delivery unless later playtesting demonstrates a specific need.

The displayed **From:** line may be misleading because sender identity is part of the fiction. The **To:** line must never lie about who received the message.

Group messages are dispatched as one synchronized event so all players are notified together. Personal messages use their own relative offsets within the section, preventing twenty simultaneous phone checks and creating staggered movement around the room.

Recommended starting cadence:

- one group message at or near the opening of each section;
- zero or one additional group message later in a section;
- one or two personal messages per character per section only when they provoke a face-to-face action;
- four to six personal messages total per character across the game;
- no message longer than roughly 80 words;
- no required in-app reply.

The host dashboard should show each section's message count, relative delivery offsets, and recipient names before activation. Once armed, the queue clearly displays sent, pending, paused, and failed deliveries.
