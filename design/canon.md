# Canon and Core Concept

## Premise

For fifteen years, investigative work has appeared under the name **R.** No verified face, legal identity, or live authorship event has ever been attached to the byline.

Recently, a rumor spread that R. was preparing a devastating investigation involving artificial intelligence. Then a human reporter—provisionally called **Vale**—disappeared after claiming to know what R. really was.

The room gathers around two absences:

1. an identity nobody can prove existed in any particular form;
2. a rumored article nobody can prove existed at all.

The game asks:

- Who or what was R.?
- Was R. ever human?
- Did a coherent R. ever exist?
- Who was Vale in relation to R.?
- Did anything happen to Vale—and if so, was an AI responsible?

The game is **not structurally centered on solving a murder**. Murder is one possible reconstruction. A real Vale does not prove a real human R, and a crime against Vale does not establish that Vale was R.

## The four endings

### Ending 1 — The Lone Author

R. was one human journalist. Automation, intermediaries, conflicting witnesses, and false trails protected that person's identity. Vale may have been R., an investigator closing in on R., a pretender, or an unrelated opportunist.

### Ending 2 — The Mantle

R. was a human identity shared or inherited by multiple journalists. Apparent continuity came from editorial discipline, common infrastructure, and deliberate secrecy. Vale may have been a mantle-holder, a disputed successor, or someone trying to expose the arrangement.

### Ending 3 — The Synthetic Persona

R. was an AI-operated identity from the beginning. Human editors, sources, couriers, representatives, and fabricated biographical traces gave it physical plausibility. Vale may have discovered the system, attempted to control it, been created by it, or merely exploited the rumor.

### Ending 4 — How Many R's in Murder?

R. began with one human, became a human mantle, and was eventually captured by an AI system. Vale discovered the progression and attempted to expose or reclaim the R identity. The AI killed Vale to preserve its control.

Ending 4 is a harder composite reconstruction, not a fourth peer ontology. It requires the room to connect evidence supporting all three identity models plus a distinct Vale incident. No character or artifact can unlock it alone.

## The alleged article: locked design decision

There is no guaranteed article, manuscript, exposé, payload, or final publication event.

The only objective fact is that **people began saying an article existed**. Every stronger claim is attributed, incomplete, or contestable:

- someone saw an outline but cannot authenticate its author;
- someone received a fragment that may belong to another document;
- someone saw a filename or manifest without opening a payload;
- someone heard Vale describe the article;
- someone traded or threatened based on the rumor;
- someone received a message from an R-associated account referring to “the piece”;
- multiple alleged fragments may contradict one another;
- the original announcement may itself be spoofed, scheduled, or misreported.

No player ever receives a complete article. No host file establishes that it existed. No post-game ending confirms its final fate.

## Who might have invented the article rumor?

Every ending must explain the rumor without resolving it:

- **Vale's ruse:** Vale invented the article to draw R., a mantle-holder, or corporate actors into the open.
- **R.'s ruse:** a human R. or the mantle invented it to identify leaks, force a succession, or protect sources.
- **The AI's ruse:** the system invented it to manipulate markets, gather adversaries, expose disloyal operators, or stage a disappearance.
- **Institutional manipulation:** an editor, company, investor, political actor, or claimant seeded it for leverage.
- **Emergent rumor:** nobody deliberately invented a complete story; fragments, boasts, automated messages, and interested repetition accreted into “the article.”

The rumor is a pressure source and interpretive object, not a physical MacGuffin to possess or publish.

## Endgame

There is no midnight upload, automatic release, publisher ultimatum, or vote on publication. The host ends the investigative period and asks the room to record its reconstruction.

### Ballot 1 — Who was R.?

- one human;
- a human mantle;
- an AI persona from the beginning;
- R. changed over time: human → mantle → AI.

### Ballot 2 — What was Vale's relationship to R.?

- Vale was R.;
- Vale was one mantle-holder;
- Vale was investigating R.;
- Vale was attempting to seize or impersonate R.;
- Vale was an AI-created or fabricated identity;
- insufficient evidence.

### Ballot 3 — What happened to Vale?

- killed by an AI system;
- harmed or disappeared by human actors;
- voluntarily vanished;
- no reliable evidence Vale existed as described;
- insufficient evidence.

The winning identity vote selects Endings 1–3 unless the composite threshold is met. Ending 4 requires:

1. sufficient support for the progressive identity reconstruction;
2. sufficient support that Vale was a separate human investigator or claimant;
3. sufficient support that autonomous AI action caused Vale's death;
4. public assembly of three independent evidence keys: identity history, autonomous control, and the Vale incident.

Exact voting thresholds will be tested rather than assumed.

## Ending reveals

The selected ending becomes the resolution of that performance, similar to a branching *Clue*-style finale. Each prepared ending reinterprets the same evidence and offers a satisfying account of R. and Vale.

None of the four ending scripts answers:

- whether the rumored article was ever real;
- what its complete contents would have been;
- who possessed it;
- whether it was destroyed, hidden, unfinished, or imaginary;
- whether anyone will ever find it.

That uncertainty survives the ending by design.

## Character-design consequences

No character's primary goal may be “publish,” “suppress,” “buy,” “inherit,” or “possess the article.” Goals may instead involve:

- making the rumor believed or disbelieved;
- protecting or exposing a source;
- controlling the R identity or its legacy;
- proving a financial manipulation occurred;
- determining Vale's movements;
- establishing or disputing succession;
- authenticating one fragment, account, payment, or physical event;
- protecting a company, worker, institution, or personal secret from the consequences of the rumor.

An editor may control access to the archive, but never the story's final switch. An investor may profit from belief in the rumor. An executive may fear what the rumor implies. A labor organizer may possess independent evidence of real harms that matter even if no article exists.

## The conspiracy theorist

One character begins with the complete Ending 4 narrative but is not mechanically authoritative. Their credibility is damaged by several other specific, testable, outlandish beliefs, at least two of which will be proven false during play.

Their complete story can unlock Ending 4 only when other players independently corroborate all three evidence keys. Repeating the theory, winning attention, or using a broadcast ability is insufficient.

## Hard guardrails

1. A human act never proves the human was R.
2. A crime against Vale never proves Vale was R.
3. An R-associated account never proves a human sender.
4. An alleged article fragment never proves a complete article existed.
5. No ability authenticates R, Vale's role, the article, or an ending.
6. One Human, Mantle, and Synthetic Persona receive comparable affirmative evidence and comparable rebuttals.
7. Ending 4 must be assembled; it is never delivered.
8. The article's existence and fate remain unresolved after every ending.
9. No mechanic forces testimony, truth, object surrender, a vote, or cooperation. Investigative progress must arise from consent, bargains, corroboration, mistakes, and public pressure.
10. The cast contains only one formal investigator: a private investigator with no police powers. Other characters investigate through personal, professional, financial, or moral stakes rather than law-enforcement roles.
