# Competing Reconstructions of R. (host reference)

> **Status:** there is no host-only answer to whether R. was ever human. This document is a possibility map, not a true timeline. The host must never privilege one reconstruction during play or reveal one afterward as canon.

## The locked uncertainty

The same public history must support four incompatible explanations through the final vote:

| Framework | Complete reconstruction |
|---|---|
| **A — One human** | One fiercely private journalist wrote as R.; automation, intermediaries, and inconsistent witnesses protected that person's identity. |
| **B — Human mantle** | One human may have begun the byline, but multiple human authors inherited or shared it. The apparent continuity is editorial discipline and secrecy. |
| **C — Synthetic origin** | R. was an AI-operated persona from the beginning. Human couriers, editors, sources, actors, and representatives created physical traces without ever being the author. |
| **D — Institutional construct** | R. accumulated from desk copy, freelancers, automation, mythmaking, and misattributed traces. There was no single coherent journalist to disappear. |

Frameworks A and B must not receive hidden canonical priority over C and D. In particular, C must remain a complete origin story, not merely a late-stage AI takeover.

## Non-negotiable epistemic rules

1. **No evidence proves human embodiment.** A human can write a letter, collect a package, attend a meeting, or be harmed without being the authorial entity called R.
2. **No witness has identity authority.** A sincere memory of meeting “R.” proves that the witness met someone presented as R., not that the person authored the work.
3. **Provenance stops one link short.** Forensics may authenticate an artifact, device, payment, or event; it cannot authenticate the metaphysical identity behind the byline.
4. **Crime and ontology stay separate.** Players may establish obstruction, coercion, fraud, assault, or disappearance around R. without proving that the victim was R. or that murder occurred.
5. **Messages after the disappearance are multivalent.** They may be a human dead-man's switch, an AI continuing autonomously, a mantle member's schedule, or institutional theatre.
6. **The article is not a confession from a known author.** Its fragments may expose AI systems, document a struggle over the byline, or stage that struggle.

## Evidence anchors by framework

| Framework | Affirmative anchors | Required escape hatch |
|---|---|---|
| A | EV21 attributed letter; consistent style; physical routines; disputed encounter memories | None connects the physical actor conclusively to authorship. |
| B | Multiple collectors and voices; succession claim; variation in the corpus | Representatives, generated variation, or fabricated succession can explain the same pattern. |
| C | Zero-variance timing; automated messages and payments; no verified live authorship | A disciplined team using automation remains possible. |
| D | Desk-brand archive; misattributed breadcrumb trails; accreted biography | A private human, mantle, or AI could deliberately generate institutional noise. |

Every framework needs an affirmative case. “It cannot be disproved” is not enough.

## The disappearance / possible crime

The game may establish concrete acts around the disappearance: missing footage, a payment, a dead drop, an intimidated intermediary, or a person entering a building. Those facts support competing conclusions:

- a human R. was murdered or forcibly disappeared;
- a mantle-holder or source was harmed, but was not necessarily R.;
- an AI staged a human disappearance to manipulate the room or protect itself;
- an institution manufactured a crisis around a byline that never denoted one being;
- no violent crime occurred, despite real obstruction or fraud.

Even a cooperating witness can identify an act and an actor only within what they perceived. Their testimony cannot turn an unidentified person into R.

## Endgame questions

1. **Was R. ever human?** Never / one human / a human mantle / cannot determine.
2. **What best explains the fifteen-year history?** A, B, C, D, or a specifically argued hybrid.
3. **Did a crime occur around the disappearance?** What act is actually established?
4. **Was there a victim, and was that victim R.?** These are separate findings.
5. **Who bears responsibility, and for what?** Name the supported act rather than assuming murder.
6. **Is the article authentic, and should it be released?**

The host announces the room's verdict and may summarize unresolved evidence. There is no canonical solution reveal.

## Character-lens guardrail

Characters may have strong beliefs and sincere memories, but each knows only a slice. Arthur met or corresponded with someone presented as R.; Amara may have exchanged material with a source or system using R.'s channels; Ruiz investigates events around a missing identity; Manny transported objects for an unseen principal. None of these statements secretly resolves what R. was.
