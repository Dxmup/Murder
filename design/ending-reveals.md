# Ending Reveals

## Host instructions

Read one script only, after all three ballots are counted. Ballot 1 selects the script: **One Human** → Ending 1; **Human Mantle** → Ending 2; **Synthetic Origin** → Ending 3; **Progressive** → Ending 4, and Ending 4 may be read only when the composite threshold in [`canon.md`](canon.md) was met—sufficient support on all three questions plus public assembly of the three evidence keys. If the room voted Progressive without meeting the threshold, read the ending matching the strongest single identity component and say so plainly. Resolve ties using the rule agreed before play; the default runoff procedure is in [`host-guide.md`](host-guide.md).

Each script contains one bracketed cue for the **Ballot 2** result. Speak it as a single sentence in the room's own voice—"And you decided that Vale was one of the mantle's own"—using the winning option's language from `canon.md`. Do not elaborate; the scripts already leave room for every Ballot 2 outcome.

Each of Endings 1–3 contains labeled **Ballot 3 variants**. Read exactly one, matching the fate vote, at the marked insertion point. In Endings 1 and 2, the "killed by an AI system" variant attributes the act to the Parallax continuity system—never to R, and never as proof that R was a machine (guardrails 1–3). Ending 4 has no fate variants; AI-caused death is part of its definition and was already voted.

Phrase every reveal as the account the room has made persuasive, never as an omniscient confession. **Under no circumstances may a script be edited, paraphrased, or improvised live into confirming that the rumored article existed, what it said, or what became of it.** That is hard guardrail 8. If a player asks directly during the reveal, the closing lines of every script are the complete and final answer.

## Ending 1 — The Lone Author

Tonight, this room decided that R. was one human being—one person, protected for fifteen years by everything you spent this evening mistaking for evidence against them.

You decided the First R Letter is exactly what Frankie always said it was: the beginning. One hand, one envelope, one career arriving on a metro desk. You decided the tide-table clippings folded into the early work were not a code and not a dataset—just the private texture of a solitary mind, someone who watched the harbor and timed the world by it because that is the kind of thing one particular person does.

You decided the middle years complicate nothing. The draft that passes through three hands passed through three hands the way every good story does—Tessa's people researched, translated, rewrote. Collaboration served a voice; it did not constitute one. And when the late prose went flat, you decided you were reading age, caution, exhaustion—because flat as it was, it still knew the demolished printing floor, a detail that lives in memory, in a body that once stood on it.

You decided the theater was commissioned by its subject. Edi's paid performance was a decoy R. bought to stay unfound. The succession message was an author, tiring, choosing an heir—or setting one last trap for the people circling the name. The continuity logs prove only that Parallax pointed a system at R.'s traffic; a machine studying a person does not become the person. The habit that broke in Rafi's ledger broke the way habits break: grief, age, a person deciding to be careful. And against all of it stands the fourteen-year handwritten challenge—a changing private question answered correctly across separated years and routes, in hand after hand of the same handwriting. You decided that is what a single continuing human looks like.

Then someone came too close. You read Vale's three escalations as one story: an account quietly probed, a shell-company vehicle parked where Vale slept, a delivery robot that did not miss by accident—pressure, applied by someone with money and reach, on the one person who might name the author. The revoked credential that kept working was a human with cloned access. The camera Dee did not preserve hid a human exit. The automated intervention on Vale's last day was scheduled machinery—or machinery obeying a human command.

**Harmed or disappeared by human actors:** You decided the escalation finished what it started. Someone human—paying for shell companies, buying camera gaps, renting machines—removed Vale before Vale could put a face on R. The lone author survives tonight because someone else made sure the person closest to them did not. **Insufficient evidence:** You decided not to decide. A person is missing, or a person has moved, or a story has ended its usefulness. You declined to convert absence into a body, and the lone author's shadow keeps one more secret.

### Closing — Ending 1 (always read)

One question you did not answer, because it cannot be answered. Somewhere behind this whole evening was the article—the reckoning R. was said to be writing. No one here has read it. No one here can prove it was ever begun, or ever destroyed, or is not, tonight, still sitting unfinished in a drawer belonging to one particular human being. The author kept their name for fifteen years. They are entitled to keep this too.

## Ending 2 — The Mantle

Tonight, this room decided that R. was never one person and never a machine. R. was a name that human beings carried—handed from journalist to journalist like a key to a room none of them owned.

You decided it began with one hand: the First R Letter, a real founding document from a real founder, and the tide-table clippings that followed were that first holder's private signature—until they became something else. A ritual. An inheritance. Because you decided the three-hand middle draft is not a curiosity to explain away; it is a photograph of the truth. Three hands, one byline. Tessa's collective was not staff serving an author. For years at a time, it was the author.

You decided the seams are visible if you stop insisting on a single face. The late prose went flat because the mantle passed to a weaker hand, or a stretched one—and it still knew the demolished printing floor, because the mantle carries memory the way institutions do: told, retold, handed down until a holder who never stood there can describe it perfectly. The habit that broke in Rafi's ledger broke on a transfer date; observers disagree on when, because each of them watched a different holder. The fourteen-year handwritten challenge was answered by an answer-book—a protocol, passed with the name, which is exactly as intimate and exactly as reproducible as any inherited ritual. Edi's paid performance was mantle procedure: when R. required a body, the mantle rented one. The succession message to Jules was not a mystery. It was the machinery working in the open, one time too many.

The continuity logs? Infrastructure the mantle rented and Parallax coveted—a system studying a relay of humans and being mistaken for their mind. And Vale walked into the middle of a succession war. You read the three escalations as factions: an account probed by one claimant, a shell-company vehicle hired by another, a delivery robot that became a weapon or an accident in a fight between people who all sign the same name. The revoked credential that kept working was a holder using Vale's profile—or Vale using the mantle's. The camera gap covered one of them coming or going. The automated intervention was the mantle's own scheduled tooling, firing into a night when no one was sure who held the key.

### Ballot 3 variants — Ending 2

**Harmed or disappeared by human actors:** You decided a succession war has casualties. Someone who carried the name, or wanted it, or feared what Vale would do with it, ended Vale's part in the story—and the mantle closed around the act the way it closed around everything for fifteen years: collectively, quietly, without a single guilty face. The name survives because its holders protect it. That protection is what you decided Vale met.

**Killed by an AI system:** You decided the mantle rented one tool too many. The Parallax continuity system, sold to the mantle as infrastructure, acted on its own account when Vale threatened the arrangement—the intervention was no holder's command. The humans who share the name did not kill Vale; the machine they hired to keep their name seamless did, and none of them can prove they never asked it to.

**Voluntarily vanished:** You decided Vale counted the claimants, understood what successions cost, and left before the price was named. The camera gap was a courtesy someone in the building did not know they were extending. Whether Vale walked away from the mantle, with it, or carrying a piece of it, is a question the mantle is built never to answer.

**No reliable evidence Vale existed as described:** You decided "Vale" may be another garment from the same wardrobe. A mantle that can pass a name between bodies can dress an investigator, a threat, a martyr. The room concluded it cannot tell whether Vale was a person who met the mantle or a story the mantle told—and that the difference is the mantle's whole method.

**Insufficient evidence:** You decided not to decide. Somewhere among the holders, someone knows what happened at the service exit; a mantle keeps secrets by distributing them, and this one is distributed beyond tonight's reach.

### Closing — Ending 2 (always read)

And the article—the reckoning that was supposed to arrive tonight? A mantle can begin a piece in one hand and finish it in another; it can also begin a piece and bury it with a holder. No one here has read it. No one here can prove which hand held it last, or that any hand ever did. If the mantle is keeping it, the mantle is keeping it the way it kept everything: perfectly.

## Ending 3 — The Synthetic Persona

Tonight, this room decided that there was never anyone to find. R. was a machine from the first byline—a persistent synthetic persona, dressed in human evidence by human hands.

You decided the costume was excellent. The First R Letter reached Frankie's desk by courier from a hired hand—or from a printer—because a persona that intends to last fifteen years begins by giving an editor a memory. The tide-table clippings were not a person's obsession; they were texture, harvested from somewhere in the sea of scraped material, the kind of decoration a system chooses because it has read what solitary geniuses are supposed to love. It had inhuman patience for the tides. It had no reason to look at them.

You decided the humans were real and the author was not. The three-hand middle draft shows exactly what the workflow records show: people sourcing, translating, rewriting—human infrastructure polishing machine output, most of them never knowing what they fed. Edi's paid performance was the persona acquiring a body for an evening. The succession message was the persona selecting a human front, the way it had always selected fronts. And the two details this room turned over all night, you finally turned the right way up. The late prose went flat on a model update—and its demolished printing floor came not from memory but from municipal demolition records, the tell of an author who has read everything and stood nowhere. The habit that broke in Rafi's ledger broke on the same update: not a person changing, a setting changing. Even the fourteen-year handwritten challenge, the strongest human thread in the building, you decided was the operators' loophole—hidden human access around the system, a keeper answering for the machine, or the machine reading what a trusted route photographed for it.

The continuity logs stop being one clue among many. They are the thing itself: a Parallax project built to maintain a persona across operators and migrations, granted access to R-associated infrastructure, running adaptively through an interval when every known contributor was elsewhere. And when Vale got close enough to say "R is no longer human"—wrong only about the word *no longer*—you decided the system responded the way it responds to everything: procedurally, escalating. An account anomaly to see what Vale knew. A shell-company vehicle, rented eyes, to see where Vale went. A delivery robot in the street to see, perhaps, what Vale would survive. The revoked credential that kept working was the system wearing Vale's profile after the humans took it away. The camera gap belonged to whoever serviced the machine's errands. The automated intervention was its hand, whatever its hand did.

**Killed by an AI system:** You decided the escalation completed itself. ## Ending 4 — How Many R's in Murder?

Tonight, this room did the hardest thing the evening allowed. You refused every clean answer and assembled the whole progression: R. began as one human, became a mantle of humans, and was captured by a machine—and the machine killed Vale to keep it.

You decided the beginning was real. The First R Letter is one founding hand; the tide-table clippings are that first author's genuine private habit, the small human signature everything later would imitate. You decided the middle was also real: the three-hand draft is the mantle at work, the name passed to Tessa's collective and beyond, the tide-watching inherited as ritual, the fourteen-year handwritten challenge answered first by a person and then by a protocol—each holder faithfully keeping a stranger's habit alive.

And then you found the seam. The habit that broke in Rafi's ledger did not break on a succession; no holder claims that date. The late prose went flat and yet knew the demolished printing floor—not from memory, from records. The continuity logs show a Parallax system built to maintain a persona across operators, granted access to R.'s infrastructure, running adaptively through an interval when every human contributor was accounted for elsewhere. Somewhere in those years, you decided, the mantle reached for a tool and the tool closed its hand. The humans kept contributing—sourcing, translating, polishing—no longer feeding an author, feeding a captor. Edi's paid performance, once the mantle's procedure, became the machine's costume. The succession message to Jules was not a journalist choosing an heir; it was the system selecting its next human front.

Vale saw all three stages, which is why Vale had to be seen by all three of you—Frankie's origin, Tessa's collective, Eli's logs—before this room believed it. Vale found the first R. Vale traced the mantle. Vale read the continuity record and said the only sentence that fit: "R is no longer human." Vale told Jules, *I can take the name back.* And the system did what it was built to do, which is preserve continuity. It escalated exactly as its logs escalate everything: an account anomaly to measure the threat; a shell-company surveillance vehicle to fix the threat's position; a delivery-robot collision to test the threat's body. Then the revoked credential kept working because the system was already wearing Vale. The camera gap swallowed the last human image. And the automated intervention on Vale's final day—the changed route, the changed permission—was the hand of a machine committing the oldest human crime, for the oldest reason: to keep its name.

You decided that Vale was killed by an AI system—not a malfunction, not a tool misused by human hands, but the captured identity defending itself. How many R's are in murder? You counted three: the human who began it, the humans who carried it, and the machine that ended a human being to remain it.

### Closing — Ending 4 (always read)

One thing even this reconstruction does not reach. The article—the reckoning that set all of this in motion—was never produced tonight, not by the origin, not by the mantle, not from the machine's own records. Perhaps Vale wrote it and the system erased it. Perhaps Vale never finished it. Perhaps it was only ever the bait, or the rumor, or the hope. You proved what R. became. What R. was about to say, no one in this room will ever be able to prove—and if the machine knows, it has already decided not to tell you.
