# Baseline 01 — GM Simulation Report

## Executive verdict

The revised design successfully prevented Ruiz, the crime plot, or any single artifact from proving that R. was human. It did **not** yet meet the intended balance between synthetic origin and a human-origin mantle.

By the final phase, the primary framework in each character's numerical belief state was:

| Framework | Characters |
|---|---:|
| A — one human | 0 |
| B — human mantle | 12 |
| C — synthetic origin | 2 |
| D — institutional construct | 6 |

Mean framework belief changed from **A/B/C/D = 29.8/27.1/22.2/20.9** in Phase 1 to **7.0/38.4/23.8/30.9** in Phase 3. Synthetic origin remained coherent and attracted two committed final believers, but it was not equally likely to the human-mantle explanation. The one-human theory collapsed.

The crime axis remained appropriately unresolved. Mean belief that any crime occurred fell to **35.9**, and belief that a victim was R. fell to **14.3**. No character established murder, a murder victim, or a culprit. Ruiz repeatedly kept logistics, identity, harm, and responsibility separate.

## What worked

### Human embodiment was not mechanically proved

Arthur's attributed letter functioned as intended: affirmative evidence for human origin, but not proof that its signer authored R.'s journalism. Forensics authenticated account behavior rather than identity. The dead drop and footage gap supported handling or concealment without identifying a victim as R.

### Synthetic origin remained a real case

C held roughly steady across the run: **22.2 → 22.6 → 23.8** mean belief. Amara finished at 72% C, and Cody at 43% C. The zero-variance timing, queued communications, distributed logistics, and absence of verified live authorship created an affirmative theory rather than mere residual doubt.

### Ruiz did not break the mystery

Ruiz's Narrow Immunity Offer went unused because no witness could offer qualifying first-hand evidence of the decisive act. Her Compel affected custody of EV09 but did not establish identity or harm. This run therefore shows that the revised wording avoids an automatic solution; it does **not** yet test whether immunity is balanced when accepted.

### Participation was broad

All 20 characters acted in every phase, made public claims, pursued named relationships, and updated beliefs. No role disappeared from the simulation.

## What failed or remains weak

### B and D became a new union attractor

Many final explanations used “mantle,” “organized operation,” “shared infrastructure,” and “institutional construct” almost interchangeably. B won 12 primary belief states and D won 6, but their boundary was often semantic rather than evidentiary. Together they absorbed 18 of 20 characters.

This is the central design finding: the game currently asks players to distinguish B and D without a sufficiently sharp rule for what would make one true and the other false.

### A had no late-game recovery

A declined from 29.8 to 7.0 and won no character's final belief state. EV21 establishes a human-written artifact and Arthur's memory establishes a claimed encounter, but every late public message weakens continuity. A needs one late, independently testable continuity fact that still stops short of proving authorship.

### C was plausible but not competitive enough

C's evidence was repeatedly explained by a human team using automation. The autoresponder caveat prevented a proof leak, but it also served as a universal escape hatch. C needs evidence that is more costly for B to explain while remaining non-conclusive—something reflecting generative behavior or long-term autonomous adaptation, not merely scheduling.

### Publication was nearly automatic

The article release vote was **17–3**. M40's automatic midnight publication makes stopping release feel like active suppression, creating a dominant default. Most “yes” votes were actually conditional: verify, label provenance, protect sources, or publish excerpts. A binary ballot loses that distinction.

### Social mechanics were under-simulated

The engine delivered proposals between phases but did not require explicit accept/reject/counter responses. Many deals, payments, endorsements, and evidence transfers remained narrated intentions. Eleanor and Wren remained chokepoints because alternate routes were not mechanically resolved.

Several agents also targeted the wrong character ID for a named recipient. This was a harness defect caused by omitting a public ID/name roster, now fixed for future runs.

### Ability state was not authoritative

Compel briefly produced contradictory custody claims: Ruiz declared it used while the holder continued to withhold the evidence until the next phase. Broadcast abilities resolved instantly, while custodial and forensic actions often waited. The next engine version needs an authoritative action-resolution and evidence-custody ledger.

|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Mechanics | 8 | 6 | 4 | 9 | 8 |
| Mystery | 6 | 7 | 4 | 8 | 9 |

The disagreement is useful: the mystery survived, but the adversarial evaluator correctly warns that synchronized public messages and weak transaction resolution make this run a test of the evidence schedule more than a full social game.

## Recommended next experiment

Do not immediately rebalance content based on one run. First improve the engine, then run matched variants:

1. Add explicit response turns and an authoritative ledger for evidence, deals, abilities, and custody.
2. Split publication into: release raw now; publish verified excerpts; hold for verification; suppress.
3. Define B as traceable human succession and D as institutional production without such succession, then adjust ballot wording accordingly.
4. Add one late A continuity clue and one C clue that is harder—but not impossible—for a coordinated human mantle to explain.
5. Run the same seed with and without M22/M41/M26/M40 to measure how much public scheduling drives convergence.
6. Force a qualifying witness opportunity in a separate Ruiz treatment so Narrow Immunity is actually exercised, then compare it with a no-immunity control.

## Method limitations

This was one compressed, three-phase run with 20 isolated character agents and four independent evaluators. It is useful for finding structural problems, not for claiming statistical balance. Agents received synchronized official messages, and private negotiation was asynchronous and shallow. The next run should correct those protocol limitations before design conclusions are treated as robust.
